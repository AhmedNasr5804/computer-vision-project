01 · TuSimple Class Balance

Why class weighting and synthesized stop matter

  • Straight ~60%: highway footage is dominated by long straight segments.
  • Right ~22% / Left ~19%: roughly balanced gentle curves.
  • Stop ~0%: natural highway frames almost never have zero ego-lanes — we synthesize stop at training time by zeroing the lane region on a 10% random subset.
This imbalance later drives a real failure mode: without augmentation and with a short EarlyStopping patience, six of nine controlled-experiment cells collapsed to the majority predictor. We will look at the recovery in Section 4.

02 · Eye pipeline overview

Three candidate families, one head-to-head

Why three families?

The rubric demands a classical-CV reference (so we know an SVM with HOG is the floor) and a from-scratch CNN (so any TL gain is measurable as marginal lift rather than a black-box win). The lightweight family explores whether the same architectural template scales down without losing the ceiling.

Same protocol everywhere

Identical 70/15/15 split, identical CLAHE preprocessing, identical seed (42). All candidates feed the same model-selection table in Section 5.

02 · Eye Classical Baseline

HSV + CLAHE + HOG + Linear SVM

Why each step

  • Grayscale + CLAHE: eye-state is a texture / edge problem; chroma is uninformative on CEW (mixed BMP / JPEG). CLAHE locally equalizes the eye-socket area so the SVM sees the gradient pattern rather than absolute brightness.
  • Canny (Otsu-adaptive): edge map of the upper face. We tune the threshold from Otsu's bimodal split so the same recipe works on bright and dim images.
  • HOG: 8×8 px / cell, 2×2 cells / block, 9 orientations. Sampled directly from the published CEW baselines so we sit on the literature floor, not below it.
  • Linear SVM with GridSearchCV C∈{0.1, 1, 10}. Linear so the result is reproducible and interpretable.
Final test accuracy 0.874 at 14 k SVM parameters. This is the floor the CNN must clear — and it does, by only 2.2 pp.
02 · Eye Custom CNN

From-scratch architecture  25.8 k params

Why this shape

  • 3 conv blocks, widths 16 / 32 / 64: a power-of-two ramp, doubling channels at each pool. Standard image-classification ramp.
  • BN after every conv stabilizes training on a 2 k-sample dataset, lets us push LR to 1e-3 without divergence.
  • ReLU + 2×2 MaxPool: cheap and translation-equivariant, well within the int8 op set.
  • Global Average Pooling instead of Flatten: kills the 4 k-parameter dense layer a naive design would introduce, and behaves like a regularizer for free.
  • Dense(32) + Dropout(0.5): head capacity calibrated to the dataset size (~25 examples per parameter).
  • Dense(2) softmax: 2-class softmax instead of a single sigmoid for symmetric probability calibration.

Adam lr=1e-3, ReduceLROnPlateau, EarlyStopping(patience=8). No pretrained weights anywhere — required by the rubric.

Input 64x64x1 (grayscale + CLAHE) | Conv(16, 3x3) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool -> 32x32x16 Conv(32, 3x3) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool -> 16x16x32 Conv(64, 3x3) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool -> 8x8x64 | GlobalAvgPool2D -> 64 Dense(32) + ReLU + Dropout(0.5) -> 32 Dense(2, softmax) -> 2 | P(closed), P(open)
02 · Eye Custom CNN

Training behaviour & calibration

What the curves show

Convergence inside ten epochs; train-val gap stays under 0.05 across the run. The dropout-plus-GAP head is delivering the regularization we asked it to. The characteristic L-shape on the val loss (fast drop, then oscillating plateau) is consistent with the EarlyStopping cut at epoch 30.

Confusion + ROC

Confusion matrix is near-symmetric and very slightly favours recall on OPEN. ROC AUC ~0.96. The residual errors concentrate on partially-open eyes that sit on the decision boundary — a binary frame structurally cannot disambiguate them.

02 · Lane Classical (TuSimple)

HSV-S + ROI + Canny + Hough + SVM

Stage-by-stage rationale

  • Downsample 1280×720 → 640×360 and crop to the lower 60% ROI. Sky and horizon contribute zero lane information and only add Canny noise.
  • HSV S-channel fused with grayscale: lane markings are saturation-dominant. Mixing in V keeps the white-on-asphalt lane visible under shadow.
  • Canny: edge map of the ROI after a slight Gaussian blur.
  • Probabilistic Hough: lines clustered into left vs right groups by slope sign.
  • 30-D feature vector: per-side line statistics (mean slope, mean intercept, count) plus a 12-bin angle histogram.
  • One-vs-rest linear SVM over four classes (left / right / straight / stop).
02 · Lane Custom CNN (TuSimple)

From-scratch encoder  ~86 k params

Why this shape

  • Four encoder blocks, widths 16 / 32 / 64 / 96: deeper than the eye CNN because TuSimple frames have road scenery, sky, vehicles — many more useful mid-level features.
  • 128×128 RGB: keeps aspect-ratio close to 16:9 after a single letter-box crop; small enough to fit in a Pi-class latency budget.
  • BN + ReLU + 2×2 MaxPool after every block. Standard, int8-friendly.
  • GAP + Dropout(0.4) + Dense(4) softmax head.
  • Class-weighted categorical cross-entropy because of the 60 / 22 / 19 / 0 distribution.
  • Adam 1e-3, ReduceLROnPlateau, EarlyStopping. No pretrained weights.
The 0.13 train-val gap is the largest of the project, reflecting the heavier distortion of the lane task — not overfitting per se, but the inherent ambiguity of single-frame curvature buckets.
02 · Lane TL (TuSimple)

MobileNetV2 backbone — the only place TL wins

Why TL wins here but lost on the eye task

TuSimple frames contain real road scenery — sky, vehicles, lane markings on asphalt. ImageNet's low- and mid-level features (edges, textures, vehicle parts) are genuinely transferable. The MobileNetV2 fine-tune lifts test accuracy by ~7 pp over the from-scratch CNN. The model-selection algorithm still chooses the lightweight baseline because the latency / size penalty (8.3× larger, 9.5× slower) outweighs the 3 pp gain under the lane scoring weights.

The Pi deployment artifact does not inherit any weights from this MobileNetV2 fine-tune — the Pi track in Section 6 is trained from scratch on regenerated supervision. The TuSimple TL number is the canonical-benchmark result mandated by the rubric.

03 · Eye Robustness

Eye from-scratch CNN under synthetic distortions

Distortion suite (test-time only)

  • Brightness ±20 / ±40 %
  • Rotation ±10° / ±25°
  • Scale 0.8× / 1.2×
  • Occlusion 25% rectangle + 8×16-px cutout
  • Gaussian noise σ ∈ {5, 15, 25}
  • Motion blur k ∈ {5, 15}
  • JPEG q=30 + barrel distortion k=0.2
Brightness shifts and motion blur dominate the degradation (−0.15 to −0.25 acc). Gaussian noise is relatively benign because CEW images are themselves grainy. Mean robust accuracy 0.662.
03 · Eye Misclassifications

Where does the eye CNN fail?

Failure mode is structural, not noise

The eight highest-confidence misclassifications all share one property: the eye is partially open or the subject is glancing sideways. In a binary frame the network has no class for these — it must pick one of two extremes when the truth is in between.

A three-class extension (open half closed) would likely absorb these errors. We do not pursue it in this project because the CEW labels themselves do not provide that level of granularity.

Reading the figures

04 · Failure modes documented in the paper

Majority-class collapse & the recovery prescription

Symptom

Six of nine controlled-experiment cells on the lane pipeline initially settled at the majority-class predictor: accuracy = 0.534, macro-F1 = 0.174. Diagnosis: the class-weighted training schedule (epochs=18, patience=6) was too short for the model to escape the ~10-epoch flat region near initialization. Under the same schedule, the data-augmented variants survived.

Recovery prescription

epochs=35   patience=12   5×10-4 warmup LR. All six candidates recovered to the 0.74–0.77 range.

Reported rather than hidden because it is a real failure mode of class-weighted training on imbalanced multi-class problems. It also explains why any augmentation is essential in Exp-A: light augmentation introduces enough input variability to break the model out of the flat region in fewer epochs, eliminating the need for the long warmup schedule.

05 · Selection algorithm

The six-criterion weighted score

Inputs per candidate

MetricSourceDirection
acc_cleantest-set accuracyhigher better
acc_robustmean across Exp-C distortionshigher better
f1_macrotest-set macro F1higher better
paramsmodel parameter countlower better
size_mbint8 .tflite sizelower better
latency_msS24-Ultra benchmark (eye) / x86 calibrated (lane)lower better

Weighting

eye_score  = 0.35*acc + 0.20*acc_robust + 0.15*f1
           + 0.15*(1 - lat_norm) + 0.10*(1 - size_norm) + 0.05*(1 - params_norm)

lane_score = 0.25*acc + 0.20*f1 + 0.15*acc_robust
           + 0.25*(1 - lat_norm) + 0.10*(1 - size_norm) + 0.05*(1 - params_norm)

Eye weights tilt toward accuracy (NPU-class target). Lane weights tilt toward latency (no NPU on the Pi). All non-accuracy fields are min-max-normalized over the candidate set so the score is on [0, 1].

05 · Eye Fine-tune Recipe

44 photos, 20 epochs, 4.2 s of CPU compute

Procedure (notebook eye/07 · script _finetune_44.py)

  • Auto-orient each capture by voting over four 90° rotations and choosing the one with the largest detected face.
  • Crop face to 100×100 with 5 % padding for eyebrows; resize to 64×64; BT.601 grayscale; CLAHE 8×8 clip 2.
  • Manual labels via ipywidgets → artifacts/eye_finetune_labels.json (44 entries, 22 open / 22 closed).
  • Fine-tune: load eye_winner.keras, Adam lr=1e-4, batch 4, 20 epochs, sparse-CCE, no augmentation.
  • Quantize: PTQ-int8 with 200-image CEW representative set, full int8 op set, int8 input / output dtype.
Before-fine-tune 0.568 on the 44 captures; after 1.000. Cost: 4.2 s of desktop CPU. PTQ-int8 retains both numbers within 0 pp.

05 · Truncated U-Net architecture

Why two encoder blocks are enough

Input 128x128x3 RGB (49 152 floats) | Conv(8, 3x3) + BN + ReLU e1: 128x128x8 MaxPool 2x2 p1: 64x64x8 | Conv(16, 3x3) + BN + ReLU e2: 64x64x16 MaxPool 2x2 p2: 32x32x16 | Conv(24, 3x3) + BN + ReLU b: 32x32x24 | UpSample 2x2 + concat(e2) c2: 64x64x40 Conv(16, 3x3) + BN + ReLU d2: 64x64x16 | UpSample 2x2 + concat(e1) c1: 128x128x24 Conv(8, 3x3) + BN + ReLU d1: 128x128x8 | Conv(3, 1x1, softmax) out:128x128x3

Why this shape on this domain

  • Only 2 pooling stages on a 128×128 input give a 32×32 receptive field at the bottleneck — already covers the largest lane curvature in the Pi rig.
  • No deeper layers because the Pi domain has no high-level features — the discriminative signal is a single dark-vs-cream contrast.
  • BatchNorm everywhere because the model trains from scratch on 7 361 images at batch 32 (heavy enough to make BN statistics meaningful).
  • U-shape skip connections preserve the lane edges that the encoder's 2×2 pools would otherwise blur.
  • Final head is a 1×1 conv with softmax over 3 channels — bg left right.

06 · Why PTQ-int8

Quantization comes after training, not during

The procedure

  • Calibrate with a 200-image representative dataset drawn from the training split (eye: CEW; lane: regenerated Pi masks).
  • Convert with tf.lite.Optimize.DEFAULT + TFLITE_BUILTINS_INT8; input & output dtype set to int8 so the runtime never has to dequantize at the I/O boundary.
  • Verify by re-running the test split through the int8 interpreter and comparing IoU / accuracy.

Why not QAT

QAT (quantization-aware training) would fine-tune the network with simulated int8 quantization in the forward pass. We tried it via tensorflow_model_optimization; with our TFMOT version it failed on the cloned-model graph (documented in the notebook). PTQ alone already retains accuracy to within 0 pp on both pipelines, so QAT's marginal gain is not worth the additional fragility.

Supervisor's directive: train first to preserve features, optimize second. PTQ matches this; QAT would not.

06 · S24 Ultra benchmarks

What the three TFLite delegates actually do

Reading the bars

  • CPU XNNPACK (4 threads): 0.126 ms — the optimum at this model size. XNNPACK fuses int8 conv-BN-ReLU into ARM NEON kernels.
  • GPU (Adreno OpenCL): 1.100 ms — slower than CPU because OpenCL kernel-launch overhead dominates at this tiny op count.
  • NNAPI: 0.125 ms — identical to CPU because the delegate falls back to XNNPACK at runtime (next slide).
~7 900 FPS theoretical for the 64×64 classifier on the S24 CPU. In the live app the bottleneck is ML Kit face detection (~1 ms median end-to-end), not our model.
06 · NNAPI is a dead end

Why the Hexagon NPU is unreachable from this APK

The TFLite benchmark runtime accepts --use_nnapi=true, constructs the delegate, then logs:

I tflite  : Created TensorFlow Lite delegate for NNAPI.
W tflite  : Though NNAPI delegate is explicitly applied, the model graph
            will not be executed by the delegate.

The runtime then silently falls back to XNNPACK. This behaviour is consistent with prior reports for Samsung's OneUI on Android 15 +: the Hexagon vendor driver is not exposed via NNAPI. Reaching the NPU requires Qualcomm's AI Engine Direct (QNN) SDK, which the stock benchmark APK does not bundle.

Mobile deployment on this hardware is therefore CPU-bound until the project migrates to a QNN delegate — documented as the recommended path forward in the paper.
06 · Live Android app

Kotlin / CameraX / ML Kit / TFLite / Firebase

Per-frame pipeline (~30 fps, ~1 ms end-to-end)

  1. CameraX ImageAnalysis → YUV → RGB upright bitmap.
  2. ML Kit face detection (FAST mode, no landmarks).
  3. Square crop, +30 % padding, −10 % y-shift — matches CEW composition.
  4. BT.601 grayscale + 8×8 CLAHE in pure Kotlin (two-pass clip+redistribute mimicking cv2.createCLAHE).
  5. TFLite int8 classifier — auto-detects dtype and dequantizes via the model's quantization params.
  6. EMA smoother α = 0.25 + Schmitt-trigger hysteresis 0.62 / 0.38 on P(open).
  7. Firebase RTDB push at 10 Hz to /eye_monitor.
06 · Live Android app · UX details

Two things that mattered in practice

CLAHE matters and is not trivial off-the-shelf

TFLite ships no Java / Kotlin CLAHE primitive. We re-implemented OpenCV's cv2.createCLAHE(2.0, (8,8)) in pure Kotlin (96 lines, two-pass clip-and-redistribute, bilinear inter-tile interpolation).

A naive global percentile-stretch alternative gave a 44-pixel-mean error against OpenCV's output and produced a model bias of ~95 % P(closed) on any face it saw.

Single-frame predictions are too noisy for a stable UI label

An EMA with α = 0.25 (effective ~230 ms window) on per-frame P(open) plus a Schmitt-trigger hysteresis (require P(open) > 0.62 to flip to OPEN, < 0.38 to flip back to CLOSED) eliminates the label flicker that occurs when raw predictions hover near 0.5.

Same machinery as a noise-tolerant comparator in analog electronics — the wider band trades a tiny bit of responsiveness for monotonicity.

06 · Firebase RTDB

How the phone hands the Pi a fresh decision

Schema written to /eye_monitor

{
  state       : "OPEN" | "CLOSED" | "UNKNOWN",
  p_open      : 0..1,
  p_closed    : 0..1,
  latency_ms  : double,
  fps         : double,
  timestamp   : long  (ServerValue.TIMESTAMP),
  device_id   : "<Build.MODEL>_<android_id>"
}
  • Programmatic init via FirebaseOptions.Builder — no google-services.json required.
  • Throttled to 10 Hz inside FirebaseClient.push() so a 30-fps camera doesn't flood the DB.
  • Server timestamp — monotonic regardless of phone clock drift.

Verify from any shell

Re-run repeatedly and the state flips with the camera. The Pi side polls this endpoint at 10 Hz with the same cadence as the publisher.

  • Widths 8 / 16 / 24 (not 64 / 128 / 256 / 512).
  • 128×128 input (not 256×512 or 1280×720).

Key Insights

Four things that survive past this project — the ones we expect to be re-cited.
07 · Insight 1

Lightweight wins at small N

Quantified

CEW has 2 423 samples. MobileNet candidates carry 1–2 million parameters, more than two orders of magnitude beyond the dataset's information budget for binary classification.

  • cnn_from_scratch — 0.896 at 25.8 k params.
  • mobilenetv2_finetune — 0.885 at 2.26 M params.
  • ~88× larger model, 0.011 less accurate.
The cross-over likely sits at ~50 k labelled examples. Below that, custom small networks beat off-the-shelf transferred giants on accuracy and latency.
07 · Insight 2

CEW does not transfer zero-shot, and the fix is cheap

CEW's collection conditions (mostly clean-shaven, mostly East-Asian, mostly forward-looking subjects, mostly indoor sodium-lamp lighting) are very different from typical 2026 smartphone-selfie conditions (beards, headphones, mixed lighting, oblique gaze toward a laptop screen). The recovery prescription is unspectacular: 44 labeled photos of the eventual users, four seconds of desktop-CPU fine-tune, no architectural change.

This is the kind of domain gap a literature-reported ≥95 % on CEW does not forewarn you of. The per-user fine-tune is a useful template for any drowsiness-detection product targeting non-East-Asian or non-clean-shaven drivers.
07 · Insight 3

Mask regeneration is mandatory when the segmentor is out of distribution

The story in numbers

  • SegFormer-Cityscapes on Pi test-bench: median lane coverage 0.8 %, range 0.0–13.6 %.
  • Otsu + 3×3 open + 200 px component filter: median 34.2 %, tight range.
  • Sanity overfit IoU: 0.244 → 0.992 on the same 50 images / same model / same optimizer — only the supervision changes.
  • Replacement runs at 21 ms / frame on CPU — 4 lines of OpenCV.
When a deep segmentor's training distribution is far from your deployment distribution, a four-line classical pipeline delivers mask quality no amount of fine-tuning will recover.
07 · Insight 4

Depth hurts on a high-contrast domain

Both reach effectively the same IoU on the Pi test split. The 6.8× smaller model trains in 9.8 min on desktop CPU from scratch, occupies 26.7 KB after PTQ-int8, and inherits no spurious sky / horizon / vehicle features that would later need to be unlearned.

From the supervisor's direct guidance: "You can't fine-tune a deep model trained on high-level features such as the TuSimple dataset with a trivial case such as the Pi camera dataset and expect results — you should first drop some of the high-level feature extractors."

On a domain whose information content collapses to a single dark / light threshold, depth is dead weight. The shallow network is not only smaller and faster; it is structurally a better fit for the task.
